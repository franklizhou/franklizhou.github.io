Caltech Course Projects

Microprocessor Systems Laboratory (EE52) - Blackfin MP3 Player

January 2015 - July 2016

The Blackfin MP3 Player is a microprocessor based system which is capable of playing MP3 files stored on a FAT formatted hard drive. I designed and created a fully functional MP3 player in Caltech's Microprocessor Systems Laboratory course. The MP3 player has microprocessor, display, keypad, audio output, IDE, DRAM, SRAM, and ROM blocks. Firmware was written in a mix of C and assembly for the BlackfinDSP core.

Analog Electronics Project (EE90) - Electronic Stethoscope

April 2016 - June 2016

The electronic stethoscope outputs captured sounds through a headphone jack and was designed to satisfy the requirements of Caltech's Analog Electronics Project course. The electronic stethoscope consists of a piezoelectric microphone, a fourth order differential preamp filter, an audio power amplifier, and a level sensing circuit. The user can control the volume by adjusting a potentiometer and visually determine the output levels by looking at a series of LEDs. The components are mounted on a custom PCB and the entire project is packaged in a MDF box.

Experimental Projects in Electronic Circuits (EE91) - Acoustic Communication System

October 2016 - December 2016

The acoustic communication system allows two laptops to send pings wirelessly through sound. The acoustic communication system consists of two boads. Each board has four microphone circuits, a quad-sampling ADC, a microcontroller, and a piezoelectric buzzer. Each microphone circuit consists of a microphone, a microphone preamplifier, and a fourth order differential low pass filter and the four microphone circuits are arranged in a square. The quad-sampling ADC simulatenously samples all four channels and interrupts the microcontroller. The microcontroller reads the voltage levels of all four levels and stores the data in a buffer. The microcontroller calculates the amplitude of the signal at a specific target frequency. Each time the amplitude surpasses a set threshold, the board uses the phase differences between the four channels to determine the direction of the audio signal and sends a acoustic ping at a specific frequency. The direction of the origin of the signal is reported as a 3D unit vector to the laptop over RS232. This way, once a laptop initiates a ping, the two boards will continuously ping each other and report the direction of the incoming audio signal.

Caltech Robotics Team Projects

The Caltech Robotics Team competes in AUVSI's Robosub competition. The Robosub competition is an international autonomous underwater vehicle competition that takes place annually in the six million gallon TRANSDEC pool in San Diego. Each year, the Caltech Robotics Team completely rethinks and redesigns the mechanical and electrical subsystems. In 2016, the Caltech Robotics Team won first place out of over fifty teams.

I am currently leading the electrical subteam for the 2016-2017 competition year. The projects below are modules I worked on for the 2015-2016 vehicle. The core, pneumatics, and sensor modules all feature ARM-based microcontrollers and firmware for the three modules was written in C.

Caltech Robotics Team - Core Module

October 2015 - July 2016

The core module acts as a TTL to CAN (Controller Area Network) master. The onboard computer communicates with the core module through a TTL connection and the core module manages a CAN bus to facilitate communication between the computer and the pneumatics and sensor modules. The core module parses incoming TTL packets and translates the messages to CAN packets and vice versa.

Caltech Robotics Team - Pneumatics Module

October 2015 - July 2016

The pneumatics module is responsible for actuating solenoids to trigger pneumatic valves that manipulate grippers, shoot torpedoes, and drop markers. The pneumatics module receives commands from the core module through the CAN bus and sends information about the current state of all pneumatic solenoids to the core module through the CAN bus. The pneumatics module features fourteen solenoid outputs. GPIOs from the ARM microcontroller are optoisolated and the solenoids are actuated using p-channel MOSFETs.

Caltech Robotics Team - Sensor Module

October 2015 - July 2016

The sensor module is responsible for managing incoming sensor data and controlling individually addressable LED strips that indicate the current status of the vehicle. Analog data inputs are filtered with a 100 Hz second-order Bessel low pass filter and read with ADCs and reed switch inputs are optoisolated and read with GPIOs. The sensor board reports the status of all sensor inputs to the core module through the CAN bus every 0.1s. The sensor board also received commands from the core module to control the color of each LED in the LED strip.

Caltech Robotics Team - USB to RS232/TTL Module

October 2015 - July 2016

The USB to RS232/TTL module uses a FTDI chip to split a single COM port on the onboard computer into four different serial channels. The four serial channels are used to establish communication between the computer and various sensors and modules.

Research Projects

Wireless Power Feedback for Microwave Wireless Power

March 2016 - August 2016

A microwave wireless power system uses phased arrays to wirelessly direct power over medium to long distances. The system I worked on is controlled using a computer running MATLAB scripts and sends power through transmitter antennas. In order for wireless power to be effective, the transmitter and the receiver must be able to communicate wirelessly. I developed a wireless feedback system capable of selectively optimizing power transfer to a single receiver.

The transmitter and the receiver communicate using half-duplex transceivers. Since the transceivers cannot send and receive data simultaneously, a robust communication protocol is necessary to control which transceiver is transmitting data and which transceiver is receiving data. The communication protocol I developed allows the transmitter to search for all receivers in the area, send and receive pings, and send data requests and receive data responses.

In addition to communicating with the transmitter controller, the receiver controller is responsbile for charging a connected phone or tablet. The receiver controller is powered by the connected phone or tablet, the onboard LiPo battery, or the received wireless power. The receiver controller actively monitors the voltages of the received wireless power and onboard battery to determine which is the more appropriate power source. The receiver controller also performs load switching on the received wireless power to determine if the received wireless power is enough to power receiver controller, charge the LiPo battery, and charge the connected device.

The transmitter controller acts as an interface between the computer, the transmitter antennas, and the receiver controller. The transmitter controller communicates with the computer over SPI, controls the phases of the transmitter antennas with a parallel bus, and communicates with the receiver controller through the wireless transceiver.
